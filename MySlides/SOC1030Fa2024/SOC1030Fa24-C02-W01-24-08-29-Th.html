What Is a Social Problem? Two Components

  • Social Problems - are social conditions perceived to be problematic by groups of people.
  • Two aspects of social problems
    • Objective aspect: how much harm is inflicted on the people who suffer from the social conditions
    • Subjective aspect: social problems are socially constructed.

## Social Problems Process: Two Approaches: Textbook - Stage 1: Emergence and Claims Making - claim-makers try to influence public perceptions of, the reasons for, and possible solutions to the social problem - Stage 2: Legitimacy - Persuade others (often government) that their claims are legitimate - Stage 3: Renewed Claims Making - actors may conclude that action is too limited, they may make demands again and often criticizing official responses - Stage 4: Development of Alternative Strategies - claim-makers may realize that these claims may fail to win an adequate response from established interests, they develop their own strategies to address the problem
## Social Problems Process: Two Approaches: Best - Stage 1: Claimsmaking - claimsmakers argue a troubling condition should be recognized as social problem and should be addressed - Stage 2: Media coverage - claimsmakers bring social problem to wider audience through media - Stage 3: Public reaction - the public responds in different ways to the claims made - Stage 4: Policymaking - policies/laws are changed to address the troubling conditions - Stage 5: Social problems work - the implementation/enforcement of the policies/laws meant to address the troubling conditions - Stage 6: Policy outcomes - policy/law changes are evaluated and critiqued (doesn't to far enough vs goes too far)
- Textbook: - Stage 1: Emergence and Claims Making - Stage 2: Legitimacy - Stage 3: Renewed Claims Making - Stage 4: Development of Alternative Strategies - Best: - Stage 1: Claimsmaking - Stage 2: Media coverage - Stage 3: Public reaction - Stage 4: Policymaking - Stage 5: Social problems work - Stage 6: Policy outcomes - How do these two approaches differ? - What advantages and/or disadvantages does each approach have? - Often sociologists will have different ways of analyzing the same phenomena
