## Doing Gender - **Doing Gender** is the idea that gender, rather than being an innate quality of individuals, is a psychologically ingrained social construct that actively surfaces in everyday human interaction. (West and Zimmerman, 1987) - Alternate definition: "[G]ender as something accomplished through the ongoing social interactions people have with one another" (Andersen, Taylor, and Logio 2017 p. 273) - Sex difference is the assumed biological category - Gender difference is "established and sustained by the socially required identificatory displays that proclaim one's membership in one or the other category" - West and Zimmerman "Accounting for doing gender" - Each culture has its own way of "doing gender." - Gender is a social construction - Fluidity of our gender ideal over time (pink as a "strong" color)
## Gendered Expectations and Socialization - **Gender Socialization**: The process through which we learn gender norms and expectations. - Agents of gender socialization: families, peers, education, media, religion, and sports.

## Explaining the Wage Gap - This is difficult due to the number of variables that can be relevant - Individuals will often sort into different occupations based on gender - Within an occupation, men and women often perform different kinds of jobs - Possible differences in: - Education - Choice of career - Skills - Experiences - Job responsibilities - Hours worked - Discrimination - Could be due to prejudice, implicit bias, statistical discrimination etc. - Social norms between genders - Married men being alone with women - Norms of men as breadwinners - Parental differences (these are normalized more for women than men) - Parental leave - Taking time off to care for children - Many others
## Reasons for the Wage Gap - We will cover these four reasons for the wage gap - Occupational Segregation - The Glass Ceiling - The Glass Escalator - The Motherhood Penalty
## Occupational Segregation - **Occupational Segregation**: the distribution of workers based on gender - Why are women likely to select into certain occupations? Especially in lower-paid occupations? - The supply side: gender socialization and "gendered occupation" - The demand side: Power disparity within occupation. - Historacal Examples - Primary school teachers in the U.S. was a "man's job" - Others: real estate sales, clerical work, pharmacy, public relations, bartending, bank telling, and more recently, the academic disciplines of sociology and biology
## Occupational Segregation - How does this occupational segregation happen? - Why did the historically male-dominated occupations become feminized? - Job Queues, Gender Queues (1990), Barbara Reskin and Patricia Roos - Women end up in lower-paid jobs because these occupations lose (or have lost) their attractiveness for white men. - As an occupation becomes commercialized, jobs becomes deskilled and less autonomous, earnings decline, routes of upward mobility close off, work conditions in general deteriorate. - Book editing, "a gentlemen's profession," was turned into "a virtual female ghetto" (Reskin and Roos, 1990)
## The Glass Ceiling - **The Glass Ceiling**: the institutional barriers within a workplace hierarchy that prevent women from reaching upper-level positions. - Two kinds of jobs for women in the corporation: secretarial jobs and managerial jobs (Men and Women of the Corporation, Rosabeth Moss Kanter 1977.) - A cultural conflation of authority with masculinity - The job often makes the person; the person doesn't make the job
## The Glass Ceiling - 2. gender stereotypes (a study of law firms by Jennifer L. Pierce) - Two kinds of jobs for women in the law firms paralegals and trial lawyers - Paralegals: are expected to be deferential, caring, and even motherly toward the trial lawyers - Trial lawyers: are expected to perform masculine emotional labor; but women trial lawyers are described "bitches," "obnoxious," and "shrill." - Female lawyers face in a double bind -- masculine emotional labor (aggression, intimidation, and manipulation) - "bitches," "obnoxious," and "shrill." - "too nice" and "too bashful."

The "glass-ceiling index" measures the chances of equal treatment in the workplace for women. It combines data on higher education, workforce participation, pay, child-care costs, maternity and paternity rights, business-school applications and representation in senior jobs.

## The Glass Escalator - **Glass escalator**: when men, especially heterosexual white men, enter female-dominated professions, they experience advantages in pay, promotions, and support from colleagues and supervisors. - Still a Man's World (Christine Williams 1995) - Male nurses, elementary school teachers, librarians, and social workers - Male workers maintain masculine power and privilege. - An inverse of gender escalator in fashion industry: lower pay and less job opportunities for male models. - Structural reasons: no managerial positions or other promotions for models.
## The Glass Escalator > The authors find that although boys are less likely to participate in performing arts than girls, conditional on participating, they are significantly more likely to be leaders. White boys, in particular, have a strong leadership advantage compared with all other schoolmates, and especially to girls of color. The significant disadvantage girls of color face relative to White boys in attaining leadership within feminized domains in high school parallels established patterns in the occupational sphere. (Keynton and Lee 2024)
## The Motherhood Penalty - **The Motherhood Penalty**: the loss in wages associated with becoming a mother (estimated at about 5% per child), and tend to be passed over for promotion opportunities - One possible explanation is that employers are biased against mothers - Field experiment (Correll, Benard, and Paik 2007) - They responded to hundreds of advertised job openings with fake cover letters and resumes - Manipulation on the motherhood status - Woman applicants with a young child are less likely to be called back. They are also recommended with less salaries
