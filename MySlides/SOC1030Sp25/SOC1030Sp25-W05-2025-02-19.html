## Two Types of Inequality - Gradational inequality - Relational inequality
## Gradational inequality - Jean-Jacques Rousseau - Two forms of social inequality: - **Physical (or natural)**, which “consists in a difference of age, health, bodily strength, and the qualities of the mind or the soul.” - **Social**, which “depends on a kind of conventional inequality and is established or at least authorized by the consent of men, …, consists of the different privileges which some men enjoy to the prejudice of others, such as that of being more rich, more honored, more powerful, or even in a position to exact obedience.” (Discourse on Inequality) - **Gradational inequality** = a ladder or scale of inequality: upper, upper middle, middle, lower middle, lower
## Relational inequality - Georg Friedrich Hegel and Karl Marx - Master-slave dialectic / opposing social classes - **Relational inequality** = a structure of social relations that bind together the advantaged and disadvantaged: - A position is relational in a sense that to describe that particular positions are also to describe how it is connected to other positions. - Capitalists and workers; lords and serfs.
## Views of inequality - Gradational inequality = a ladder or scale of inequality: upper, upper middle, middle, lower middle, lower - Relational inequality = a structure of social relations that bind together the advantaged and disadvantaged - (less important) Inequality is a necessary condition of abundance (Thomas Malthus)

(Keep your response to 2 sentences) - **Social mobility** is the movement between different positions within a system of social stratification in any given society. - **Horizontal mobility**: a group or individual transitioning from one social status to another situated more or less on the same rung of the ladder. - **Vertical mobility**: the rise or fall of an individual or group from one social stratum to another. - **Intergenerational mobility**: one generation moves up or down in social class compared with their previous generation. - **Intragenerational mobility**: an individual moves up or down in social class within their own lifetime. - **Economic mobility** is the ability to move up and down the economic ladder during one's lifetime and across generations. [(Pew Trusts)](https://www.pewtrusts.org/en/research-and-analysis/data-visualizations/2013/faces-of-economic-mobility) - **Social Reproduction of Inequality**: The basic idea is that social positions (e.g., social class, occupations, education, etc.) tend to pass on from one generation to another - **Social Institution**: institutions are social structures that have rules, norms and boundaries that influence the behavior of individuals. - Examples: Families, religion, government, schools, healthcare etc. - **Social Capital**: connections to people who have resources you can access - Examples: law or health expertise (doctors, lawyers), job prospects - **Cultural Capital**: class based knowledge and preferences used to signal your class to others - Examples: Opera (RBG), SNL Colbert etc., cars, etc. - **Social Closure**: group boundaries and identities that prevent others from using group resources - Examples: Country clubs, LDS humanitarian aid, Federalist Society etc. - NOTE: you only need to know the terms above that Prof. Shi has used in class (on his slides) or are in the textbook
