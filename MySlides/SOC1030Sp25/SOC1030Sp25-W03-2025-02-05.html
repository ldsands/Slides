## Theory - "a supposition or a system of ideas intended to explain something, especially one based on general principles independent of the thing to be explained" ([source](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/theory)) - Sociologists answer lots of questions but sometimes they find correlations that they didn't expect - We will be identifying aspects of these theories in everyday life - First we'll review the 3 classical sociological theoretical frameworks
## Theoretical Perspectives - Questions to keep in mind - How did founders of sociology see social problems? - How would the major theoretical perspectives analyze a social problem differently from the other major theoretical perspectives?
## Karl Marx (1818-1883) - Capitalism's most intense critic - Problems that Marx saw in Capitalism - Specialization of work alienates the meaning of the work from the workers. - Enormous gap in pay between workers and capitalists. - His writings provided the theoretical basis for communism. - The fundamental problem in a capitalist society is the **social relationship** between two classes with regards to the means of production. - The **bourgeoisie**, or ruling class, owns the means of production, - The **proletariat**, or working class, does not own the means of production and instead is oppressed and exploited by the bourgeoisie
## Conflict theory - It sees the society as imposed by different groups struggling for resources, power, status. - Studies issues such as race, gender, social class, criminal justice, and international relations. - Key theorist - Karl Marx (1818-1883); - Class-conflict theory - **Capitalism** - **Bourgeoisie** vs. **Proletariat**
## W.E.B. Du Bois (1868-1963) - An African-American sociologist - Race-conflict theory - Ethnography in the African American community - Main concept: "Double Consciousness" - The first is the script that any American would have for moving through the world; - The second is the script that takes the external opinions of an often racially prejudiced onlooker into consideration - Co-founded National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP)
## Émile Durkheim (1858-1917) - Why does capitalism make us richer and frequently more miserable? - He studied the causes of an upsurge in suicide rate in the 19th century France. - From a traditionally agricultural society to an urban industrial economy. - Le Suicide - Great economic success and high rate of industrialization - Individualism vs. Social integration - Freedom and economic boom vs. traditional ways of life
## Structural Functionalism - Society is seen as a complex system whose parts work together to promote stability and internal cohesion. - Key theorists - Auguste Comte (1798-1857) - Emile Durkheim (1858-1917) - Parsons (1902-1979) - Level of analysis - **Macro** - **Manifest** vs. **Latent** Functions - Manifest functions are intended or obvious consequences of a particular structure - Latent functions are unintended or unrecognized.

## Symbolic interactionism - It focuses on the relationships among individuals within a society, and the meaning they attach to them. Society is a product of everyday social interactions. - Key theorists: - Max Weber; George H. Mead; Erving Goffman - Level of analysis - **Micro** - Subjective meaning and **social construction** - Is something real? Vs. What makes something real? - "If [people] define situations as real, they are real in their consequences." - Examples: - Money - Hand gestures

## Structural Functionalism - Functional Structures Persist - Society has needs structures form to fill those needs - The example often used is a living organism - Émile Durkheim - suicide rates in France - Manifest vs. Latent Functions - Limitations and criticisms - Function is a value judgment e.g. sports - Integration in systems mean less freedom e.g. military, religious organizations, education
## Functionalism Example - Health Care in the US - Keep in mind Manifest vs. Latent Functions - Doctors do a majority of the diagnosis work - Due to a lack of doctors nurse practitioners and PAs have increased in number to make up for the lack of doctors - Just because this is how it happened doesn't mean it is the only way to fill a need - Insurance in the US is privatized and largely managed through employers - WWII history - companies competed via healthcare rather than pay - This is not the only way of getting insurance that works look at other countries such as Canada
## Conflict Theory - Started with Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels - Observed bad conditions in factories in the 1840 England and concluded revolt of the workers - Didn't foresee the gains made by unions and government on behalf of workers - **Bourgeoisie** vs **Proletariat** (resources, power and status) - Purdue Pharma and the Sacklers in the Supreme Court (Harrington v. Purdue Pharma L.P., ET AL.) Raided Purdue, bargaining for immunity - Immunity was denied it has gone back to negotiations - W.E.B. Du Bois - Double Consciousness - has second script that takes the external opinions of another into consideration - In-group vs out-group (us vs them) - e.g. Iowa vs Iowa State, Gonzaga vs St. Mary's, BYU - Limitations and criticisms - Frequently ignore commonalities and consensus
## Conflict Theory - Example - Utah State, Utah and BYU in the formation of the WAC - Utah State in the early 60s was beating Utah and BYU in football - This lined up with the formation of the WAC because Utah and BYU had more money they lobbied to leave Utah State out of the new conference - They did this so Utah State would cease its climb in popularity, it worked really well - Recent conference mergers - WSU and OSU losers, who else? - How does resources, power and status factor into these examples?
## Symbolic Interaction - Grew out of backlash against early Psychology (stimulus response) - Mead/Blumer added interpretation to stimulus response - Cooley the "Looking-glass self" (mirror) we see ourselves how others see us (based upon other's actions toward us) - Use symbols to interact - Uniforms (suit and tie, military ranks, my shirt, etc.) - "If [people] define situations as real, they are real in their consequences." - Limitations and criticisms - does little to address inequality, overly focused on symbols
